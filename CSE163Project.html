"Education Disparity in Washington State"¶

Research Questions and Summary¶

  1. To what extent do wealth and diversity correlate with test scores?

    Like we expected, we found that the higher a household's income is, the better their children do in standardized testing. However, unlike the relationship between income and testing standards met, there was merely a weak to no correlation between diversity and testing standards. So, to our surprise, we were not able to conclude with confidence that greater diversity leads to better testing performance.

  2. Has the difference in test scores between the richest and poorest counties increased or decreased over time?

    Overall, the difference has not greatly increased or decreased over time, although there has been variation from year to year. Despite this, there is still a significant test scores gap between the richest and poorest counties of about 15-20% that has not lessened.

  3. How wealthy does a county have to be in order to have the majority of students meet testing standards?

    The predicted income per capita has to be around 70,000 for ELA and 105,000 for math, but because of the distribution of the data, the presence of outliers, and range of error for the linear regression model, these numbers and predictions should be interpreted with caution.

Data Setting and Methods¶

Data Setting¶

Demographics Dataset: This dataset was collected by Public Health-Seattle and King County (PHSKC). The numbers in the data are population estimates and not exact. This means that we will have to trust that the estimations made by the group that sourced this data are accurate, which may lead to unknown errors and biases associated with the PHSKC.

Income Dataset: This dataset was collected by the US Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA). These numbers should be exact and quantitative data is less susceptible to bias, so this data should be reliable.

Scores Dataset: This dataset was collected by the Washington Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction (OSPI). We used a filtering function within the source website to remove some irrelevant data to make the dataset size more manageable before downloading. The main part of the data setting that could have an impact on our results are the suppression rules for how the data is presented. Small schools, private schools, and home schooled students are excluded from the data to protect their privacy. While that does leave a majority of schools' data available to us, since we do not have 100% of it our results could still be affected. The data also only measures the annual spring testing results, not grades. It could be that grades may be a better indicator of academic success, but for the purposes of our project and availability of data, we will be using spring testing results.

def school_year(year):
  """
  Given the academic calendar years, returns a integer
  of just the later year.

  >>> school_year("2021-22")
  2022
  >>> school_year("2019-20")
  2020
  """
  return int(year[:2] + year[5:7])


def percentage(percentage):
  """
  Given a string percentage, returns the float version of it.
  If the percentage has been rounded for suppression, returns the
  float of the rounded percentage.
  If there are no students, returns 0.
  If the percentage has been completely suppressed, returns nan.

  >>> percentage("51.6%")
  51.6
  >>> percentage(">90")
  90.0
  >>> percentage("Suppressed: N<10")
  nan
  """
  percentage = percentage.replace("%", "")
  percentage = percentage.replace("<", "")
  percentage = percentage.replace(">", "")
  if "Suppressed" in percentage:
    return np.nan
  elif percentage == "No Students":
    return 0.0
  return float(percentage)

def spearman_stats(merged_data):
    """
    Given the merged data frame, this function calculates and 
    prints the Spearman correlation (rho and p-value) between

Analysis¶

To analyze the plots using statistical methods, we initally intended to use Pearson's r value (correlation coefficient) to measure the strength and direction of a linear relationship between income and testing success as well as diversity and testing success. R values range from -1 (a strong negative correlation) to +1 (a strong positive correlation). The closer the r value is to 0, the weaker the relationship is. Generally, when an r value greater than 0.5 is considered statistically significant. The p value indicates how likely it is to obtain a result at least as extreme as the result you actually observe. In a nutshell, a p value less than 0.05 indicates statistical significance and that your correlation is unlikely due to chance. However, Pearson's correlation test assumes that the distribution of variables is approximately a normal distribution. When variables do not follow this normal distribution, the calculated p values may be inaccurate or misleading. As we can see, almost all the variables for Math and ELA testing do not follow a normal distribution.

Consequently, we pivoted to using the Spearman Rank Correlation test, which is better suited for non-normally distributed data. It simply describes the monotonic relationship between two variables. A rho value between 0 and 0.40 generally means weak to no correlation, one between 0.40 and 0.60 means a moderately strong correlation, and one above 0.60 means a strong or even very strong correlation. The p value in the Spearman Rank Correlation test tests the null hypothesis (the assumption that there is no correlation between the variables). If the p value is less than 0.05, this means the results are statistically significant.

The strong positive correlation and 0.0 p value for the Spearman test between income and score (for both Math and ELA standardized testing) allows us to confidently say that there is a positive correlation between the income of a household and how well those children perform in standardized testing. The correlation between diversity and testing scores is a little less obvious. We can say with confidence that there is a weak, positive correlation between diversity and ELA standardized testing score, the rho value is 0.326 (between 0.20 and 0.40), and the p value is less than 0.05. However, we cannot say with confidence that there is any negative correlation between diversity and Math standardized testing scores, as the p value is large (0.231).

So, what can we take away from these statistics? We can state with confidence that the higher a household's income is, the better their children do in standardized testing. However, we cannot conclude that greater diversity leads to better testing scores in general. We could be more specific and confidently state that increased diversity leads to higher ELA standardized testing scores, but only ELA and not Math testing scores.

There is no data for 2020 because of state testing cancellations due to Covid-19. After 2020, there is a drop in passing percentage across all groups and both subjects. Interestingly, the sharpest decline is seen in the math results for richest counties. The decline in the testing results for 2021 is most likely because of how the 2020-2021 school year was primarily online, leading to lower engagement with students and therefore lower test scores. After 2021 when most schools made the transition back to in-person school, the richest counties seem to make a recovery in results for both subjects, although not yet returning to pre-Covid levels.

In contrast, while the poorest counties did not see as much of a drastic decline in scores after 2020 compared to the richest, that could be more because the results were originally lower, not because of a particularly high-quality online school system. In 2022 with the return of in-person school, the poorest counties do not make much of a recovery, with the scores staying generally the same as the 2021 results. Lack of funding could have made making a recovery more difficult.

Implications¶

Our analysis explored how two key factors, income per capita and racial/ethnic diversity, relate to standardized test performance in Washington state. Specifically, we analyzed the relationship between these variables and the percentage of students meeting grade-level standards in Math and English Language Arts (ELA) via the Smarter Balanced assessment (SBAC).

Who might benefit from this analysis?¶

  • Policymakers could use this analysis to better allocate funding and support to schools in counties with lower test scores or have not made a recovery from Covid.
  • School administrators could use this data to make targeted interventions. Using socioeconomic and demographic contexts for students, they could provide more resources and help for certain students, potentially improving test scores and general academic performance.
  • Scholarship organizations can prioritize offering financial help to students in areas with less income

Who might be excluded or harmed?¶

  • Students from underrepresented or disadvantaged backgrounds may be unfairly characterized by numbers and statistics, reinforcing stereotyped and inaccurate narratives/assumptions if interpreted incorrectly. While the data analysis can provide a county level overview, it does not acknowledge potential disparities within counties. Within a county with a high income per capita, there could be a high level of economic and academic inequality which is obscured by the data analysis process. There is data lost when trying to get a bigger picture of a problem.
  • Schools and counties with high diversity might be stigmatized if correlations are misunderstood as causal relationships rather than reflections of systemic inequities or indicators of other societal issues. If this data is taken out of context, it could be used as a weapon against diversity instead of a tool for improving student success.
  • Since the SBAC tests are in English, counties with a higher proportion of English learners may have lower results.
  • Private schools are not included in this dataset since they may not partake in standardized testing.
  • Very small schools were not included in the data due to the suppression rules from the scores dataset due to student privacy laws.

Limitations¶

Correlation Does Not Mean Causation¶

For our statistical analysis, we used Pearson and Spearman correlation tests/values to describe associations between the variables we were testing. These do not necessarily mean that income or diversity are causal mechanisms. Although statistical analysis factors in the possibility of correlation due to chance, it is important to remember that the two variables being compared are not necessarily directly related.

Outside Factors¶

Many factors play into how well a child does in standardized testing. Variables like parental education, school funding, teacher qualifications, student-faculty ratios, and so much more all play into student testing outcomes. For this project, we simply tested two large factors, income and diversity. These are certainly influential, but it is important to remember that there are countless other confounding variables that play into academic success.

Covid-19¶

From the multiple graphs across the analysis. It is clear that Covid-19 negatively impacted test scores across all of Washington state. It is likely that even with data a couple years after Covid from 2022, there are still lingering effects reflected in the test scores. These effects could have affected the accuracy of our analysis or obscured other trends in the scores.

Considerations for Future Work¶

Wider Scope¶

Our work in this project could be used as a framework for similar analysis done in other states or on a larger scale. It is clear that there is education disparity in Washington, but we do not know how Washington compares to other states. It could be insightful to do this type of project across all 50 states to see which states need the most improvement and which states could provide an example of how to reduce education inequality.

In addition, we used results from the SBAC testing, but many schools also offer other forms of standardized testing, like the WCAS or AIM. Looking at different types of standardized testing and comparing their results against each other could reveal if certain score trends and correlations are universal across all tests or just specific to one type.

More Factors¶

We focused on income and diversity in this project, but test scores and academic achievement in general are extremely complex topics that cannot be attributed to only two variables. Some other things that could be taken into account as factors include county population, the urban vs rural divide, school sizes, access to technology, and amount of spending per student. Even intangible factors like school culture or levels of teacher/student engagement can contribute. Any of these other variables could be a stronger predictor of student testing results.

More Post-Covid Data¶

The most recent scores data we used was from 2022 and it was still affected by Covid. While the data from 2022 showed that many counties are slowly returning to pre-Covid test results, we cannot be sure that recovery with be a quick or linear process without actually looking at data from 2023 and beyond.
